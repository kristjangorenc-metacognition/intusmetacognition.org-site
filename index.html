Our Mission

We investigate how an artificial system might detect contradictions, generate hypotheses, integrate them into memory, and track how those internal structures evolve. The aim is not to replicate human cognition, but to design and experimentally study transparent, modular, and logically grounded epistemic pipelines that can explain their steps, expose internal tensions, and update their conceptual structure through cycles of self-evaluation.

Current large scale AI architectures are strong pattern generators, but they seldom represent epistemic coherence explicitly. They typically do not track how beliefs emerge, how they interact, or how they should be revised when confronted with conflict. Our research pipeline approaches reasoning as a regulated feedback process: tensions in input trigger hypothesis generation, hypotheses are tested against accumulated memory, and accepted insights reshape the system's conceptual space.

Central Question

How far can a contradiction-driven epistemic approach, equipped with structured memory and explicit reasoning mechanisms, scale toward robust, interpretable, domain-general intelligence?

Rather than presenting a finished AI system, this is a research inquiry a methodological framework for testing epistemic ideas in a controlled, inspectable way.

Foundations

This research represents a cross-disciplinary synthesis drawing from cognitive science, epistemology, systems theory, neuro-symbolic AI, computational linguistics, and formal reasoning. Initial testbeds focus on biology, physiology, pathology, and medicine domains where reasoning must be causal, precise, and resilient under uncertainty. The pipeline, which we call INTUS, situates itself within research traditions of belief revision, abductive inference, structured memory, and feedback regulation.

Core Research Areas

Our work explores several interconnected theoretical and computational challenges:

  • abductive reasoning and hypothesis generation,
  • contradiction driven inference and belief revision,
  • ontology grounded reasoning and semantic drift,
  • structural vs probabilistic approaches to reasoning,
  • epistemic stability and meta level control mechanisms.

Development Status

This is an exploratory and evolving project, built from first principles and tested iteratively across synthetic, clinical, lexical, and narrative environments. The current implementation is a working research prototype designed to test theoretical ideas about epistemic reasoning and cognitive architecture.
